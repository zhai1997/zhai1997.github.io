Algorithmic bias? an empirical study of apparent gender-based discrimination in the display of stem career ads

Category: Advertising

This paper conducts a field test, and finds that a gender neutral ad was systematically sent more to the male. After ruling out several possibilities, they conclude that it probably due to the spillover of other ads.

Introduction

  • In settings where ads are allocated by algorithm, research has documented instances where historically discriminated-against groups are more likely to be associated with undesirable ads
  • But they do not attempt to understand why ad algorithms can produce apparently discriminatory outcomes
  • Empirical focus on information about STEM careers is motivated by the fact that policy makers in many countries are concerned about a shortage of graduates in the STEM sector, particularly women
  • Providing information is important because of evidence showing that the shortage is not necessarily a result of hiring practices (Conditional on applying, women are more likely than men to be hire)
  • Distortions in perceptions about careers in STEM across genders may potentially explain why women do not apply
  1. There are some subtle differences between the expensive traffic and the cheap
    1. The ctr and pvr are high for the expensive (due to both purchasing power and less avoidance)
    2. More ads are targeted at them
    3. If no targeting ads:
      1. Due to their low pvr, the bid should be high enough to send (ecpm threshold)
    4. If targeting ads exist:
      1. More ads would target at the experience, so the bid should be high enough to win and the quality of the ads should also be high enough
    5. The success of the experiment relies on the small enough difference between the attitudes of the expensive and the cheap
    6. If the authors increase the bid, will they find the female. Yes! But there will be more male
  2. The bid should be same for a single ad or campaign
  3. It could be that at cold start periods, the female click less
