Topics of WOM

Category: Interesting things I found

Background

The literature together describes a game with four players, the producers, the reviewers, the potential buyers and the review holding platforms.

  • The information in this game is incomplete. The reviews posted are common knowledge, but the quality of the product is unknown at first, and becomes known to the buyers at different periods depending on whether it is experience good or credence good (Dulleck and Kerschbamer, 2006)

  • The reviewers are buyers possibly with extremely positive or negative feelings about the purchase (Li and Hitt, 2008), infiltrated by interested parties (Mayzlin, Dover, and Chevalier, 2014) and non-buyers (Anderson and Simester, 2014). Instead of honestly sharing their private information, they consider the social image of themselves (Schlosse, 2005), the opinions of their friends (Wang, Zhang and Hann, 2018), etc.

  • The producer can use online reviews to predict the sales (Dellarocas, Zhang, and Awad, 2007), and may probably manipulate the reviews on the platforms (Mayzlin, Dover, and Chevalier, 2014) due to certain economic incentives (Luca and Zervas, 2016).

  • The potential buyers use the reviews to infer unobservable information. The average level (Chevalier and Mayzlin, 2006) and distribution (Sun, 2012) of ratings will both influence the ratings, while the characteristics of products and customers moderate this process (Zhu and Zhang, 2010).

Historical path of the development

Change in topics

The influence of online reviews on the decision-making of customers.

  • The earlier literature solved the most fundamental issue, whether the online reviews influence the sales or not (Chevalier and Mayzlin, 2006). Without the existence of the effects of WOM on sales, many later topics are meaningless, like the economic incentives behind the manipulation of reviews. With the correlation between online reviews and sales, Dellarocas, Zhang and Awad (2007) successfully introduced the box office revenue prediction model.

  • But later as more researchers were studying the effects of WOM on sales, conflicted results were found. For example, Chen, Wu, and Yoon (2004) found the opposite result to that of Chevalier and Mayzlin (2006). Zhu and Zhang (2010) proposed a convincing reconciliation to the mixed results of previous studies. They concluded that the characteristics of products and consumers moderate the influence of online reviews on sales. A lot of later researchers followed this study to find factors that moderate the effect of ratings, like Rosario et al.(2016)

  • In addition to the studies on moderating effect, with the development of online review platform, more and more metrics were provided to customers, like the distribution of ratings, the details about the quality, etc. Some researchers began to study the influence of the newly published information on sales. For example, Sun (2012) studied the interactive influence of variance and mean of ratings.

When we know consumers actually rely on online reviews to make a decision, some researchers are worried about the usefulness and quality of online reviews.

  • Manipulation by producers: Studying the fake reviews manipulated by producers is important, but empirically challenging, because the fake reviews are designed to be unobservable. Mayzlin, Dover, and Chevalier (2014) developed a novel method to detect the existence of review fraud without the data of fake reviews. Luca and Zervas (2016) further studied the economic incentives behind the manipulation of online reviews.

  • Social influence of friends: Why people become similar to one another after they build a social relationship is a popular topic in the studies about Social Network. As some online review platforms embedded the network function, Wang, Zhang and Hann (2018) studied the social influence when reviewers leave the ratings, which undermines the efficacy of online reviews.

  • Reporting bias: Researchers found the bias of reporting (Reichling 2004) quite early, and Dellarocas and Wood (2008) were the first to quantify the extent of this bias and its influence.

  • With the systematic bias and distorted reviews, Gao, et al.(2015) compared the consumer generated online reviews with the offline rating data, and found that despite no reviews for physicians with low patient-perceived quality and exaggerated reviews for physicians with high patient-perceived quality, overall the online reviews correctly reflect the perceived quality by patients.

Change in methodology

  • Different factors influence the review generating process and sales, DID method is widely used by the literature to eliminate the unobservable factors

    • DID model was first adopted by Chevalier and Mayzlin (2006) and then Sun (2012) to eliminate the book fixed effects and book-site fixed effects.
    • Zhu and Zhang (2010) also took the competition into consideration, so they introduced the characteristics of other games into the model, and also they used DID method to eliminate unobservable variables.
    • Mayzlin, Dover, and Chevalier (2014) changed the conventional DID model. They differenced types of hotels rather than time.

  • With the deepening of researches, the relationship between variables are more and more complex, which makes theoretical model important in understanding the impact path. Zhu and Zhang (2010), Sun (2012), Mayzlin, Dover, and Chevalier (2014) also developed a theoretical model. The models in Sun (2012) and Mayzlin, Dover, and Chevalier (2014) are similar, in that they both assumed the quality is unknown to anyone and consumers’ tastes belong to a taste space as a straight line.

  • Later researchers studying the social influence and review fraud are facing more challenges when designing empirical models, which requires them to creatively choose the research context and carefully examine the validity of data.

    • Wang, Zhang and Hann (2018) used post-consumption reviews to eliminate observational learning, and comparison between friends who saw the salient reminding with ones who did not to eliminate homophily effects.
    • Luca and Zevas (2016) used the filtered reviews by algorithms as the proxy and collected a second data set to do cross-validation.

Possible research issues

WOM in other forms, like Videos and Lives

  • Actually, WOM does not have to be in textual forms. With the development of technology, we can find WOM on many network platforms like Instagram, Douyin, WeChat Moments, etc. I believe on those platforms, we can find something new:
    • The distinction between advertising and WOM is subtle on social network platforms. According to the traditional definition, on Douyin Live, if a celebrity recommends a product to his followers only in order to help them make more rational purchases, we call it WOM. If he gets paid for it, we call it advertising. But in most cases, he does both in Douyin Live, and even he gets paid for the promotion, he will try to make sure the product is good enough to maintain his reputation. Followers also do not care if the celebrity gets paid for the promotion.
    • The WOM generating process is more complex on social networks than on online review holding platforms. People can chat about the purchasing experience, argue on disagreements, and try the products in front of the camera.

The difference of the average ratings and difference in user structures across platforms

  • Schoenmueller et al. (2020) studied the different user structures of different platforms, which reminds me that a lot of papers mentioned the difference in average rating of a single product across platforms, but few studies explored this issue. I think there are three possible explanations:
    • Different platforms have different users.
    • The same users with different perceptions about the rating scales of different platforms: One consumer holding a fixed opinion about a movie may give different stars on different platforms. For example, he may think 4-star is enough to tell others this is a good movie on Douban, but 4-star only means average on Taopiaopiao.
    • Manipulation by platforms: Some platforms (especially those selling products) want to make profits, so they may use some tricks to improve the overall ratings of movies. Studying the economic incentives of review holding platforms is also useful to understand this issue.

Adoption of Cheap Talk theory to model how WOM influence potential users

  • I find researchers used to develop the theoretical model where the consumers have perfect information in the last stage. But actually the reviews they have are severely biased and distorted. How will they discount the usefulness of reviews is interesting. Economists developed a theory called Cheap Talk to model giving and receiving advice with no cost of advice (Sobel, 2013). I guess it will be helpful.

Do online reviews add to the polarization of products

  • Reingen et al. (1984) found offline WOM made people living together share the same preference about products. On the Internet, people are all living together. Do online rating platforms make the popular movies more popular, and aggravate the revenues of less popular movies?
