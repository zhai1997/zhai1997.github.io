Field Experiments in Marketing

Category: Empirical Methods

This is a guide to field experiments.

Why a field experiment?

  1. The empirical gold standard in the social sciences is to estimate a causal effect of some action
  2. A field experiment ensures that ex ante, via random experiment, any differences between the treated and control group should not matter

Decide on unit of randomization

  1. At the highest level of generality, the statistical power of a randomized experiment is likely to increase with greater granularity of the unit of randomization. The lack of granularity in randomization reduced the chance that ex ante the “unobserved ways” do not matter
  2. There are also constraints that argue against granularity
    1. There are constraints imposed by the cost and logistics of having finely-grained unit of observation
    2. The researcher needs to minimize the potential for spillovers and crossovers between experimental treatments
  • “Optimizely” allows researchers to independently and easily run field tests to evaluate the effects of different landing pages or website content

Ensure no spillover and crossover effects

  1. A spillover occurs when a treated individual affects the outcomes for other untreated individuals. One possible solution is to randomized at the level of plausibly isolated social networks such as a community, rather than randomizing at the level of the individual
  2. A crossover occurs when an individual who was supposed to be assigned to one treatment is accidentally exposed to another treatment

Decide on complete and stratified randomization

  1. In stratified randomization, individuals are first divided into subsamples based on covariate values so that each of the subsamples are more homogeneous relative to that covariate than the full sample. Then each individual in each of the subsamples is randomized
  2. The stratified technique is useful if a covariate is strongly correlated with an outcome.
  3. In marketing research, reliable data which allow such stratification may not be present.
  • Do we need to divide audience into several groups?
  • Stratified randomization is another way of control.

Ensure that appropriate data is collected

  1. This is particularly important in digital environments where different parties have access to different types of data and it is not always obvious how these can be collected and linked
  2. Researchers should carefully consider data points that are not directly linked to measuring the outcome of the randomization, because they may help the researcher understand the behavioral mechanism or rule out alternative interpretations
  3. Researchers should be aware of the potential need for a large sample when conducting a field experiment, especially when the magnitude, direction and heterogeneity of the treatment effect are unknown
  • What other data sources can we access?

Interpreting results from a field experiment

  1. The key issue is understanding exactly what is different between the groups who were treated
  2. In general, researchers should try and collect data for as long a period as possible to understand wether any treatment they measure is stable, dissipates or increases in its effect over time
  3. The existence or importance of Hawthorne effects, where the mere fact of being observed as part of a field experiment can alter outcomes, is the subject of much academic debate. The researcher should provide assuring evidence for the reader that the causal effect they measure between the treatment and control is associated with the effect they claim
  4. Firms may only be willing to experiment with, in their view, less successful media or sales territories, and to incur costs of experimentation for their best customers
  • How long should we conduct our experiments?
  • What other effects can our treatment leads to?

Lambrecht, A., & Tucker, C. E. (2015). Field experiments in marketing. Available at SSRN 2630209.
