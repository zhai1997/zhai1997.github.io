When and Why Do Buyers Rate in Online Markets?

Category: Fake reviews

This paper studies the generation of online reviews using Bayesian methods. The core assumption is that the buyer is more inclined to leave a review when the absolute difference between his Bayesian posterior and prior is larger. The model based on this assumption successfully predicts the reality and generates meaningful simulation results.

Model

Basic Setup

  • Only consider one typical buyer.
  • The transaction experience is a signal $s\in\{ g,b\}$, where $s=g$ if the transaction is good and $s=b$ if it is bad.
  • Sellers can be of high or low quality $q\in \{q_h, q_l\}$. The seller type is not known to the buyer.

Before the transaction

  • The buyer has initial belief $\lambda \in (0,1)$ that sellers entering the platform are of good quality.
  • She updates this belief using the specific sellers public reputation record, which is summarized by an index $y$.
  • To update her initial belief, the buyer specifies probability $\sigma_h (y)=\mathrm{Pr}(y|q=q_h)$ and $\sigma_l (y)=\mathrm{Pr}(y|q=q_l)$. Then the buyer forms a Bayesian belief
    $$ \mu (\lambda,y)=\frac{\lambda\sigma_h (y)}{\lambda\sigma_h (y)+(1-\lambda)\sigma_l (y)}$$
  • Assumption 1: $\sigma_h(y)/\sigma_l(y)$ is strictly increasing in $y$. This assumption guarantees that a better rating record increases the belief while a worse one decreases it.

After the transaction

  • Assume the transaction happens for sure.
  • After the buyer receives the signal $s$ about the seller’s quality. Let $\rho^h\equiv\mathrm{Pr}(s=g|q=q_h)$ and $\rho^l\equiv\mathrm{Pr}(s=g|q=q_l)$.
  • Assumption 2: $\rho^h>\rho^l$ and $\rho^h>1/2$. This assumption means that the signals from transactions are informative.
  • The buyer uses the signal to to form a posterior belief $\mu^s$ about seller quality.
    $$\mu^g\equiv\mathrm{Pr}(q=q_h|s=g)=\frac{\mu\rho^h}{\mu\rho^h+(1-\mu)\rho^l}$$
    $$\mu^b\equiv\mathrm{Pr}(q=q_h|s=b)=\frac{\mu(1-\rho^h)}{\mu(1-\rho^h)+(1-\mu)(1-\rho^l)}$$
  • Let $d\equiv |\mu^s-\mu|$. The buyer leaves a rating if
    $$u(d,c)\equiv b(d)-c\geq 0$$
  • This is the core assumption in the model. $d$ is the amount of knowledge that consumers learn from the transaction. The motivation to post the information is increasing with the increase in the value of $d$. Here, $b$ can also capture the baseline inclination to leave a rating.

Analysis

  • Dependence of the change in beliefs on the rating record.
    • If the transaction experience was positive, then there exists a unique $\hat{y}$ such that the difference between posterior and prior beliefs increases in $y$ if $y<\hat{y}$, and decreases in $y$ if $y>\hat{y}$. Furthermore, $\mu(\hat{y})\equiv\hat{\mu}<1/2$.
    • If the transaction experience was negative, then there exists a unique $\check{y}$ such that the difference between prior and posterior beliefs increases in $y$ if $y<\check{y}$, and decreases in $y$ if $y>\check{y}$. Furthermore, $\mu(\check{y})\equiv\check{\mu}>1/2$.
    • Just take the first derivative, and it is easy to prove.
    • It shows that when the when $\mu$ is sufficiently high, the buyer learns little from any kind of messages.
  • Rating dynamics for a high quality seller.
    • Corollary 1: Let $\mu>\check{\mu}$ and consider a sequence of increases in $y$. Then the likelihood that a buyer derives a benefit from rating the transaction decreases and shrinks to $b(0)$ as $\mu\to 1$.

      I have question with this corollary. I think the rating of the high quality firms should be high from the very beginning and shouldn’t have such a systematically increasing trend.

  • Interim ratings and their effect.
    • Study the effect a negative feedback has on the likelihood that ensuing buyers share positive and negative experiences.
    • This setting can help guarantee that sellers don’t have enough time to strategically respond to the previous ratings.
    • The negative rating shock induces an increased probability to rate when $y>\check{y}$.
    • Let $f_1(y)\equiv\mu^g(y)-\mu(y)$, $f_2(y)\equiv\mu(y)-\mu^b(y)$, and $z(y)\equiv f_1(y)-f_2(y)$. Then,
      $$\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d} y}z(\mu(y))>0$$
    • Corollary 2: Let $y_t$ get large. Suppose that interim ratings lead $y_t$ decrease from $y_t$ to $y_{t+1}$. Let the buyer incorporate the interim decrease in the rating score in her decision. Then,
      • the likelihood that she rates the transaction increases, relative to not incorporating that shock in her rating decision.
      • the likelihood that a given rating is negative increases more relative to the likelihood that it is positive

Empirical Evidence

Empirical Challenges:

  • Seller quality is unobservable and may change over time: Construct a balanced panel of sellers and control for seller fixed effects.
  • There may be selective matching, which means that for a given seller early buyers are different from later buyers: Use rich information in the data to construct measures of buyer behavior, which will be further used a controls.
  • Transaction quality as perceived by the buyer may change over time, for instance because the seller learns, or because he changes the prices: Use alternative measures in both auction and posted price formats to show that consumer surplus does not change much over time.
  • There could be transient shocks to transactions quality, and the shocks cause negative ratings in sequence: Use an empirical strategy based on negative ratings that are left because the buyer accidentally left the wrong feedback.
  • Sellers may react to receiving bad ratings: Exploit transactions that took place before the negative feedback arrives, but the rating of this transaction happened after the arrival.
