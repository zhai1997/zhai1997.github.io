Reciprocity and unveiling in two-sided reputation systems: Evidence from an experiment on Airbnb

Category: Fake reviews

Through a large-scale field experiment, the study reveals the causal effect of one particular design choice - the timing with which feedback by one user about another is displayed on the platform. Reputation systems are widely used by nearly every digital marketplace to reduce information asymmetry and moral hazard. However, since the creation of accurate reviews is voluntary and costly, reviews are under-provided, leading to missing information and a variety of biases. These make the design of effective reputation system important for digital platforms.

Literature Review

  • Literature about why people submit feedback and whether this voluntary process produces bias.
    • Avery et al. (1999) show that evaluations will be under-provided in equilibrium without an appropriate payment scheme.
    • Miller et al. (2005) show how to design a scoring system with accurate reporting of feedback in equilibrium.
    • Dellarocas and Wood (2007) argue, using data from eBay, that people with worse experiences are less likely to submit feedback.
    • Later research finds that people with more extreme experiences are more likely to review.
  • Other factors that contribute to biased reviews
    • Li and Hitt (2008) argue that early buyers may have different preferences than late buyers, which could cause early previews to be non-representative.
    • Filippas et al. (2018) argue that because reviewers may feel bad hurting a counterparty via a negative review, average review scores may inflate over time on platforms.

Theoretical Framework

The authors add a new component “the desire to unveil reviews” to the theory of reciprocal reviewing. This component provides an incentive for agents to review more often and more quickly in the simultaneous review reputation system.

Traditional Theory

  • Prior literature covers following items in the utility function of reviewers:
    • An intrinsic cost (or benefit) from leaving a review. This will vary across individuals so that some people dislike reviewing whereas others like it.
    • A disutility from misrepresenting the quality of a transaction in a review. This implies that absent other forces individuals review honestly.
    • For a second review, a positive utility from submitting a review with a reciprocal rating. For example, the second reviewer may feel obliged to leave a positive review once they read the positive review fof the first reviewer.
    • A benefit from having more and better reviews. Because the first review affects the rate and type of second reviews, the reviewer may feel obliged to leave a positive review once they read the positive review of the first reviewer.
  • Analysis of the incentives of reviewers:
    • The second reviewer:
      • The second reviewer knows that her review will not change anything about the first review. As a result, term 4 drops out.
      • In the SR (simultaneous reveal) system, the second reviewer does not know the content of the first review. So, term 3 drops out. However, in the RIT system, term 3 remains.
      • Overall, because term 3 increases the utility of reviewing, the utility of reviewing is lower in the SR system and the review rate is predicted to fall.
    • The first reviewer:
      • The first reviewer in the SR system knows that the content of the first review will not affect the content of the second review. As a result, the first reviewer has less incentive to review and to do so positively in the SR system than in RIT system.
      • Overall, if the only factor driving reviewing behavior were reciprocity, then first reviewer’s review rate would be predicted to fall as well.

New Theory

  • Desire to unveil reviews: The presence of a “hidden” first review that can be revealed increases the utility of reviewing.
    • Because it is natural for people to be curious about others’ comments.
    • Because if people expect the review is positive, they may also want it displayed publicly as soon as possible, which may bring more bookings in the future. (strategic)
  • The key consequence of the desire to unveil reviews is that there will be more reviews and faster reviews in the SR system.
  • It may also have an effect on the speed of first reviews, because users may want to write a quick first review to trigger a quick second review. But this effect is smaller than that on the second reviews.

Empirical Work

  • They use a two-sided platform, on which the guest and the host each have the opportunity to review one another.
    • In the control group, reviews are revealed both to the counterparty and to the public as soon as they are submitted.
    • In the treatment group (simultaneous reveal), reviews are hidden until both parties have reviewed or until the time to review has expired.
  • Effects on timing and rates:
    • The SR treatment reduced the time to review by 17% for guests and 9.9% for hosts. It also increased the review rates by 1.7% for guests and 9.8% for hosts.
    • Whereas the treatment decreased the time from check-out to first review by 9.7%, it decreased the amount of time between the first and second review by much more (35%).
    • The change in review timing is seen for guests and hosts across all levels of experience.
  • Effects on the types of reviews
    • The treatment decreased the correlation between guest and host ratings by 48%.
    • The ratings in the treatment groups were more negative, but the effects were small.
  • Selection effects
    • Using methodology of principal stratification, they show that the treatment changed the review behaviors of in individuals who would have reviewed regardless of the treatment.
    • This finding supports that the effects on ratings reflect more accurate reviews in the treatment group.
  • Effects on adverse selection
    • No effects are found from the data.
    • The lack of detectable effect is because of the small overall effect of the treatment on realized distribution of ratings.
